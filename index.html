Women in strength sports

(Olympic) Weightlifting is the only stregnth based sport that's part of the Olympic games. Hence some often call it 'Olympic Weightlifting' to differentiate weightlifting from other strength-based sports like Powerlifting. CrossFit's exploding popularity, or maybe [notoriety](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=etOnWu9Qlss&pp=ygUcY3Jvc3NmaXQgZmFpbHMgZWxnaW50ZW5zaXR5IA%3D%3D), have increased interest and participation in strength-sports overall along with social media channels dedicated to featuring weightlifters training and competing. Named after how weightlifters will hold onto the barbell when performing the two competition lifts, [hookgrip](https://www.instagram.com/hookgrip/) is *the* weightlifting brand. Think Gymshark but for competitive athletes rather than influencers. Brands like hookgrip and [ATG](https://www.instagram.com/atginsta/) (short for ass-to-grass meaning your hips going as low as possible, resulting in your ass touching the metaphorical grass floor when squatting) are pivotal in bringing weightlifting to the gym-going casual public like myself. Who these pages chose to feature can impact an athelete's career, both during and outside of their competing days. Think getting brand sponsorships, building a large enough audience to cultivate into potential clientele to offer coaching courses[^1](toroktiy.com) when their careers are over. As weightlifting becomes more accessible to the public, featuring a more diverse range of atheltes is critical in promoting the sport among under represented groups. Among women, the stigma remains that touching a weight will instantly turn them into a female version of Arnold Schwarzenegger. Watchinng the amazing female atheletes on hookgrip or atginsta shows a much different picture. Weightlifters come in all different shapes and sizes. And no, you won't instantly become bulky from lifting weights. Trust me I've tried, it's really hard!
